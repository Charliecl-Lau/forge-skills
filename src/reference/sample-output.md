**Assignment:** Design a simply supported steel beam to carry a uniformly distributed load.
**Course:** Civil Engineering — Structural Analysis (3rd year)

---

<context>
You are assisting with a structural engineering assignment on steel beam design. The beam is simply supported with a span of 8 meters and carries a uniformly distributed load of 25 kN/m (including self-weight). Design uses AISC LRFD methodology. Steel grade is A992 (Fy = 345 MPa, Fu = 450 MPa). Assume full lateral bracing.
</context>

<task>
Select an appropriate W-shape steel section for this beam. Specifically:

1. Calculate the factored load using LRFD load combinations (assume the 25 kN/m is already the factored load for simplicity)
2. Determine the maximum bending moment and shear force
3. Calculate the required section modulus
4. Select the lightest W-shape that satisfies both bending and shear requirements
5. Check the selected section for deflection under service loads (limit: L/360), assuming service load is 18 kN/m
</task>

<constraints>
- Use LRFD resistance factors: φ_b = 0.90 for bending, φ_v = 1.00 for shear
- Assume compact section and full lateral bracing (no lateral-torsional buckling check required)
- Select from standard AISC W-shape tables
- Show units in SI (kN, m, MPa, mm) throughout
- Do not use beam design software or pre-computed selection charts — work from first principles using section properties
</constraints>

<verification>
- Verify the maximum moment using the standard formula M = wL²/8 and confirm the value by checking equilibrium (sum of moments about a support)
- Confirm the selected section's plastic section modulus Zx exceeds the required value with a clear margin statement
- Check that the shear capacity φVn exceeds the maximum shear force Vu
- Compute the actual deflection and express it as a fraction of span length — compare against the L/360 limit
- Identify what would change if lateral bracing were provided only at midspan instead of continuously
</verification>

<output_format>
Structure your response as:

1. **Given information** — summarize inputs and assumptions in a compact list
2. **Load analysis** — factored loads, moment diagram description, maximum moment and shear values
3. **Section selection** — required Zx, candidate sections considered, final selection with justification
4. **Checks** — bending capacity check, shear capacity check, deflection check (each as a pass/fail with numerical comparison)
5. **Summary table** — single table with columns: Check | Required | Provided | Status
6. **Limitations** — at least two simplifications made and their potential impact on the design
</output_format>

<reasoning_guidance>
Work through this problem in stages rather than jumping to section selection:

1. Start by establishing the load path and drawing the expected moment and shear diagrams conceptually — this grounds the numerical work
2. When selecting a section, explain why you chose the lightest adequate section rather than an oversized one (engineering economy reasoning)
3. If the deflection check fails for your first selection, explain the tradeoff between increasing section size and the cost/weight implications
4. Distinguish between ultimate limit state checks (moment, shear) and serviceability checks (deflection) — they serve different purposes and use different load levels

If any aspect of the problem statement is ambiguous, state your interpretation before proceeding.
</reasoning_guidance>
